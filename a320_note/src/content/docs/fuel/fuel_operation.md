During the walk around, you must check several items which are part of the fuel system.

These items are not major components but a check of their condition is very important.

Fuel can be measured manually by the magnetic fuel level indicators installed as follows:
- One under the center fuselage
- Four under each wing inner tank
- One under each wing outer tank.

![alt text](image-23.png)

We are looking at a magnetic fuel level indicator under the right wing inner tank. It should be flush with the aircraft surface.

![alt text](image-24.png)

Water drain valves are installed as follows:
- Two under the center fuselage
- One under each wing inner tank
- One under each wing outer tank
- One under each wing surge tank.

![alt text](image-25.png)

We are looking at the water drain valve in the right inner tank. You should check that there is no water leaking from the valve.

If there is a leak call maintenance.

![alt text](image-26.png)

A surge tank is connected to the outer tank in each wing tip. These small tanks protect the system against overpressure and thermal expansion.

You cannot monitor or control surge tanks from the cockpit.

The surge tank inlet should be clear of any blockage. The fuel ventilation overpressure disk should be intact.

Note: The disk is intact when a white painted cross is in view.

![alt text](image-27.png)

![alt text](image-28.png)

A refuel panel is located on the fuselage side beneath the right wing.

You should check that the REFUEL panel access door is closed.

![alt text](image-29.png)

![alt text](image-30.png)

A single refueling point is installed under the right wing.

You should check that the refuel coupling door is closed.

Note: Another refueling point can be installed under the left wing as an option.

![alt text](image-31.png)

There is also a shroud drain mast, which drains fuel that could leak from the system.

You should check that there is no fuel leaking from the mast.

The items related to the fuel system during the walk around have all been seen.

The items related to the fuel system during cockpit preparation have all been seen.

We will now discuss items related to the fuel system that you will encounter during engine start.

The indications and starting procedures are the same for both engines.

In our example we will start engine 2 first.

![alt text](image-54.png)

Before we start the engine observe the following:

- The engine LP valves are cross-line amber to indicate closure
- The fuel used quantities remain from the previous flight
- Engine identification numbers are amber (This is because the engines are not running)
- The optional Fuel.FLOW 1 + 2 indicates amber crosses.

![alt text](image-55.png)

Engine 2 is starting, observe:

- The LP valve is in-line green
- The fuel used indication automatically resets to 0
- The fuel flow starts
- The engine identification number 2 changes to white
- The Gross Weight (GW) has now appeared.

![alt text](image-56.png)

Engine 1 is starting, observe:

- The LP valve is in-line green
- The fuel used indication automatically resets to 0
- The engine identification number 1 changes to white.

![alt text](image-57.png)

The message "CTR TK FEEDG" has appeared on the EWD. This indicates that the center tank pumps are feeding the engines.

Note: As the inner tank pumps have a sequence valve, the center tank pumps when they are also running deliver fuel preferentially, as long as slats are retracted,

![alt text](image-58.png)

The center tank pumps automatically stop at slats extension.

Then it is time to stop the APU if it is not required during taxi and take off.

![alt text](image-59.png)

We will now discuss items related to the fuel system that you will encounter during normal climb, cruise and descent.

![alt text](image-60.png)

You are in climb, the slats are now retracted.

The CTR TK FEEDG message reappears indicating that the center tank pumps are now feeding the engines.

Note: The center tank must be emptied first.

![alt text](image-61.png)

You are now in cruise.

Notice the CTR TK FEEDG message has again disappeared indicating that the center tank pumps have stopped. However the center tank is not empty and another automatic function has occurred.

To explain why, we must look at the IDG cooling fuel recirculation system.

![alt text](image-62.png)

Some of the fuel supplied to each engine is used to cool the oil system of the Integrated Drive Generator (IDG), then returns to the related outer tank. If the outer tank is already full, it overflows to the related inner tank.

When the inner tank reaches the high level, the related center tank pump is automatically stopped. So, the engine is fed by the inner tank until it consumes 500 kg, and so on.

![alt text](image-63.png)

![alt text](image-64.png)

![alt text](image-65.png)

![alt text](image-66.png)

Then, 5 minutes after the center tank has been emptied, the center tank pumps are automatically stopped.

![alt text](image-67.png)

![alt text](image-68.png)

You are in descent.

To better illustrate the remaining indications, we will keep the ECAM FUEL page displayed.

Let's see what happens when the inner tanks reach a low level.

![alt text](image-69.png)

When either inner tank reaches a low level limit, the transfer valves on both sides simultaneously open enabling the transfer of fuel from the outer to the inner tanks.

![alt text](image-70.png)

When at least one transfer valve is open, the message OUTER TK FUEL XFRD is displayed on the EWD.

![alt text](image-71.png)

As you can see, the fuel is transferring until the outer tanks become empty.

Note: When the outer tank is empty, the related fuel tank temperature is not displayed.

![alt text](image-72.png)
